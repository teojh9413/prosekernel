# GitLab — Postmortem of database outage of January 31

## Source
- Author: GitLab
- URL: https://about.gitlab.com/blog/postmortem-of-database-outage-of-january-31/
- Rights note: Metadata and original analysis only. Link to the original postmortem rather than reproducing it.

## Why this is good
The postmortem is valuable because it chooses specificity over reputation management. It names what happened, what users lost, what failed inside the system, and what GitLab changed afterward. The structure earns more trust than a polished apology would.

## Craft moves
- Leads with the actual failure and user impact.
- Gives chronology so readers can understand causality.
- Separates human action, system weakness, and organizational response.
- Includes uncomfortable details instead of smoothing them into PR language.
- Converts apology into prevention commitments.

## Structure map
1. Incident summary and impact.
2. Timeline of what happened.
3. Root causes and contributing factors.
4. What went well / what went badly.
5. Remediation and future prevention.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: GitLab publicly explained a serious database outage by documenting the timeline, causes, impact, and prevention work. The lesson is that crisis writing must be useful before it is reassuring.

## Reusable pattern
For incident communication: “Here is what happened. Here is who was affected. Here is the timeline. Here is what failed. Here is what we changed. Here is how we will update you.”

## Imitation prompt
Write an incident postmortem using GitLab’s trust-repair structure. Be specific about impact, timeline, cause, responsibility, and prevention. Avoid legal fog and empty empathy.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- “We take this seriously” without facts.
- Hiding the timeline because it is embarrassing.
- Treating prevention as a vague commitment instead of a concrete change.
