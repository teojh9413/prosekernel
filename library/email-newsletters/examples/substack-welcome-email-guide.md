# Substack — Writing a good welcome email

## Source
- Author: Substack
- URL: https://on.substack.com/p/writing-a-good-welcome-email-on-substack

## Why this is good
A welcome email is not a generic greeting. It is the moment a reader decides whether this publication has a clear promise, a human sender, and a reason to stay subscribed. The useful craft here is expectation-setting: what the reader will get, how often, why it matters, and what to do next.

## Craft moves
- Opens with the relationship: why the reader is receiving this and what they joined.
- States the publication promise in concrete terms.
- Sets cadence and expectations so the reader feels oriented.
- Gives one low-friction next action instead of a menu of asks.
- Sounds like a person inviting a reader into a habit, not a brand blasting a list.

## Structure map
1. Greeting and recognition of the subscription moment.
2. One-sentence promise: what this newsletter helps the reader do or understand.
3. Cadence and content expectations.
4. Optional personal note that creates trust.
5. One next action: reply, read a starter post, or whitelist the sender.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: a strong welcome email turns subscription into a relationship. It names the value, sets expectations, and gives the reader one simple next step.

## Reusable pattern
“Thanks for joining [publication]. You are here because [reader desire/problem]. Every [cadence], I will send [specific thing] so you can [outcome]. Start with [one action].”

## Imitation prompt
Write a welcome email for this newsletter. Make the promise specific, set expectations, include one human detail, and end with one next action. Do not use launch hype or creator-brand clichés.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Saying “I’m excited to announce” before explaining reader value.
- Asking for too many actions in the first email.
- Making the newsletter sound broader than it is.
