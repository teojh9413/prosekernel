# Paul Graham — Maker's Schedule, Manager's Schedule

## Source
- Author: Paul Graham
- URL: https://paulgraham.com/makersschedule.html
- Rights note: Metadata and craft analysis only. Link to original essay.

## Why this is good
The piece works because it creates a portable distinction. “Maker schedule” and “manager schedule” become labels readers can use immediately to explain a frustration they already feel but may not have named.

## Craft moves
- Names two opposing worlds in plain words.
- Makes a private pain socially legible.
- Turns one scheduling problem into a theory of work.
- Uses a distinction as the hook, not a flashy opening.

## Structure map
1. Name the distinction.
2. Explain one side in familiar detail.
3. Contrast with the other side.
4. Show the collision.
5. Turn the distinction into a rule for action.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: makers and managers experience time differently; meetings that seem cheap to managers can destroy the productive blocks makers need.

## Reusable pattern
“People think the conflict is X. The real conflict is between [world A] and [world B].”

## Imitation prompt
Create a viral social post around this idea by naming two hidden modes of operating, showing how they collide, and ending with a practical rule. Do not copy Graham’s phrasing.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Copying the exact label structure without a real contrast.
- Making the post merely anti-meeting instead of explaining the mechanism.
