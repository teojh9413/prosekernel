# Of Experience

## Source
- Author: Michel de Montaigne
- URL: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/3600
- Rights note: Public-domain source; Humanprint stores analysis and source link.

## Why this is good
Montaigne teaches the essay as exploration. The authority is not tidy conclusion; it is the visible movement of attention across observation, self-knowledge, contradiction, and judgment.

## Craft moves
- Lets the writer’s mind remain visible on the page.
- Uses personal observation as evidence for broader reflection.
- Allows digression while returning to the central inquiry.
- Treats uncertainty as intellectual honesty.
- Finds general insight through ordinary bodily and social experience.

## Structure map
1. Begin with an observed problem or proposition.
2. Turn it against personal experience.
3. Follow relevant digressions that test the idea.
4. Admit contradictions or limits.
5. Arrive at a tempered insight rather than a slogan.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Montaigne’s essaying demonstrates reflective intelligence: the prose shows a mind testing ideas against lived experience instead of pretending thought arrived finished.

## Reusable pattern
For an exploratory essay: “I began with [idea]. Experience complicates it through [observation]. Another angle is [digression]. What remains true is [tempered conclusion].”

## Imitation prompt
Write a personal essay in the Montaigne mode. Let thought develop visibly, use concrete experience, and allow uncertainty without becoming shapeless.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Using digression as an excuse for no structure.
- Pretending uncertainty is depth by itself.
- Ending with a neat slogan that erases the exploration.
