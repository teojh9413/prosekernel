# Frederick Douglass — What to the Slave Is the Fourth of July?

## Source
- Author: Frederick Douglass
- URL: https://www.pbs.org/wgbh/aia/part4/4h2927t.html
- Rights note: Public-domain speech; this file stores analysis, not full text.

## Why this is good
Douglass uses contrast as a blade. He accepts the audience’s stated ideals, then turns those ideals back on the country’s contradictions.

## Craft moves
- Grants the value before exposing the violation.
- Uses direct address to prevent emotional distance.
- Builds from civic celebration to moral indictment.
- Makes hypocrisy visible through contrast.

## Structure map
1. Acknowledge the occasion.
2. Establish shared principles.
3. Introduce exclusion.
4. Contrast celebration with suffering.
5. Force moral recognition.
6. End with conviction rather than comfort.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: the speech asks what national celebration means to people excluded from the freedom being celebrated.

## Reusable pattern
“You celebrate [value]. But for [excluded group/user], the lived reality is [contradiction]. Therefore [moral/strategic implication].”

## Imitation prompt
Use Douglass-style contrast to expose a gap between a company’s stated promise and the user’s lived experience. Keep the stakes proportionate.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Using outrage without shared premise.
- Inflating minor product issues into moral drama.
