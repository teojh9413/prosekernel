# MLK — Letter from Birmingham Jail

## Source
- Author: Martin Luther King Jr.
- URL: https://kinginstitute.stanford.edu/letter-birmingham-jail
- Rights note: Metadata and analysis only. Link to source.

## Why this is good
The letter is a masterclass in audience-aware persuasion. King answers specific critics, establishes moral authority without sounding defensive, and moves from local complaint to universal principle.

## Craft moves
- Addresses the stated objection directly.
- Builds ethos through patience and specificity.
- Uses layered structure: personal position, moral principle, historical context, call to conscience.
- Balances restraint with urgency.

## Structure map
1. Identify why he is responding.
2. Answer the legitimacy objection.
3. Explain the moral framework.
4. Refute moderation and delay.
5. Expand from event to principle.
6. Close with dignity and pressure.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: the letter argues that direct action is justified when negotiation fails and injustice demands visible tension.

## Reusable pattern
When answering critics: name their objection fairly, explain your standing, define the deeper principle, then show why delay is itself a choice.

## Imitation prompt
Write a public response to cautious critics using this structure: respectful address, legitimacy, moral principle, refutation of delay, broader consequence, dignified close.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Borrowing moral intensity for trivial topics.
- Copying biblical cadence without equivalent stakes.
