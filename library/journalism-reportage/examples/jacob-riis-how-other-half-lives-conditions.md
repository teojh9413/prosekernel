# How the Other Half Lives

## Source
- Author: Jacob A. Riis
- URL: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/45502
- Rights note: Public-domain source; ProseKernel stores analysis and source link.

## Why this is good
Riis’s work shows journalism as exposure of conditions. It brings readers into spaces they do not see, using scene, description, and accumulated evidence to make neglect politically and morally legible.

## Craft moves
- Makes invisible environments visible through detail.
- Uses place as evidence.
- Connects individual scenes to structural conditions.
- Creates moral pressure without relying only on assertion.
- Turns observation into reform urgency.

## Structure map
1. Introduce the hidden condition or place.
2. Enter through concrete scenes.
3. Accumulate details that show the pattern.
4. Connect scenes to public responsibility.
5. Point toward reform or accountability.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Riis models conditions reporting: the writer turns spaces, rooms, and daily facts into evidence of a larger system.

## Reusable pattern
For conditions reportage: “Enter [place]. Show [details]. Let those details reveal [pattern]. Name who benefits or neglects it. Ask what public responsibility follows.”

## Imitation prompt
Write a reported conditions piece in Riis’s structure. Use place-based detail, avoid abstract outrage early, and let scenes build the case.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Describing poverty or harm voyeuristically.
- Making reform claims before evidence accumulates.
- Reducing people to scenery for the argument.
