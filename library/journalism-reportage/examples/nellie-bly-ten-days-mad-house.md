# Ten Days in a Mad-House

## Source
- Author: Nellie Bly
- URL: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/59899
- Rights note: Public-domain source via Project Gutenberg; Humanprint stores analysis and summary.

## Why this is good
Bly’s reporting works because it puts the reader inside an institution instead of merely denouncing it from outside. The authority comes from witnessed detail: rooms, procedures, conversations, and the gap between official purpose and lived reality.

## Craft moves
- Uses immersion to replace abstract accusation with observed evidence.
- Builds credibility through concrete sequence: what happened first, next, then after.
- Lets small procedural details reveal systemic failure.
- Balances first-person experience with public-interest purpose.
- Converts scenes into accountability without losing narrative drive.

## Structure map
1. Assignment or entry premise.
2. Method: how the reporter gains access.
3. Scene-by-scene observations.
4. Pattern recognition: individual events reveal institutional logic.
5. Public claim: what must change.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Bly enters an asylum under cover, observes conditions firsthand, and turns experience into an indictment of institutional treatment. The reporting persuades through scene density and procedural detail.

## Reusable pattern
For investigative reportage: enter through one person or scene, document the process step by step, let details accumulate, then name the system those details expose.

## Imitation prompt
Write a reported feature using Bly’s method: show the institution through scenes and procedures before making the argument. Use firsthand detail, named actors when available, and a clear public-interest question.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Starting with outrage before the reader has evidence.
- Replacing observation with adjectives.
- Treating the reporter’s experience as the point rather than the lens.
