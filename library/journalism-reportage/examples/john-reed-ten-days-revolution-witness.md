# Ten Days That Shook the World

## Source
- Author: John Reed
- URL: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/3076
- Rights note: Public-domain source; Humanprint stores analysis and source link.

## Why this is good
Reed’s reportage is useful for its immediacy. The writer tracks events as lived experience: crowds, rooms, arguments, uncertainty, and movement. The craft lesson is preserving momentum without losing political context.

## Craft moves
- Uses scene density to convey historical movement.
- Tracks sequence under uncertainty.
- Names actors and factions to orient the reader.
- Balances atmosphere with event chronology.
- Lets urgency come from proximity to events.

## Structure map
1. Establish the historical moment and stakes.
2. Enter through scenes and actors.
3. Follow rapid events chronologically.
4. Pause to explain factions or context.
5. Return to the momentum of events.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Reed demonstrates eyewitness political reportage: events are explained by moving through them, with enough context to prevent chaos from becoming confusion.

## Reusable pattern
For fast-moving reportage: “Scene: [where we are]. Actors: [who matters]. Event: [what changes]. Context: [why it matters]. Next scene: [new development].”

## Imitation prompt
Write eyewitness reportage using Reed’s momentum. Preserve scene, sequence, and stakes; explain context only when needed for orientation.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Letting atmosphere replace factual sequence.
- Dropping names without orienting the reader.
- Flattening chaotic events into retrospective certainty.
