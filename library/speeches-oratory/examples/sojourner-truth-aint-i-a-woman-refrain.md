# Ain’t I a Woman?

## Source
- Author: Sojourner Truth
- URL: https://sourcebooks.fordham.edu/mod/sojtruth-woman.asp
- Rights note: Public-domain source; ProseKernel stores analysis and source link.

## Why this is good
The speech’s craft power is the refrain as cross-examination. A repeated question turns the audience’s assumptions back on themselves while lived evidence prevents the argument from becoming abstract.

## Craft moves
- Uses repeated question as argument and rhythm.
- Contrasts stated beliefs with actual treatment.
- Grounds claims in embodied experience.
- Keeps pressure on the audience’s categories.
- Combines plainness with moral force.

## Structure map
1. Name the category or claim under dispute.
2. Offer lived evidence that contradicts exclusion.
3. Repeat the central question or refrain.
4. Escalate through additional contradictions.
5. End with a demand for recognition or justice.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Sojourner Truth’s address models the power of refrain: one question accumulates moral pressure as each example exposes contradiction.

## Reusable pattern
For a hypocrisy-exposing speech: “You say [principle/category]. Yet [lived evidence]. If that is true, then [refrain/question]. Repeat until the contradiction is impossible to ignore.”

## Imitation prompt
Write a speech using a repeated moral question. Anchor each repetition in concrete evidence and let the refrain become the argument.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Using repetition decoratively.
- Turning lived experience into generic identity language.
- Explaining the contradiction before the audience feels it.
