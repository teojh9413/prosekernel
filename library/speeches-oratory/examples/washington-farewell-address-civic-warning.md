# Farewell Address

## Source
- Author: George Washington
- URL: https://avalon.law.yale.edu/18th_century/washing.asp
- Rights note: Public-domain source; Humanprint stores analysis and source link.

## Why this is good
The address works as a transfer of judgment. Washington is not merely saying goodbye; he is packaging hard-won civic principles for future moments when the audience may forget them.

## Craft moves
- Uses farewell as permission for sober counsel.
- Frames warnings as protection of shared inheritance.
- Connects unity, institutions, and conduct.
- Speaks beyond the immediate audience to future decision-makers.
- Turns personal departure into communal responsibility.

## Structure map
1. Announce departure or transition.
2. Reaffirm gratitude and shared identity.
3. Name principles worth preserving.
4. Warn against predictable dangers.
5. Entrust the audience with continuation.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Washington’s address is a model for legacy speechwriting: departure becomes a moment to codify principles and warn against future failure modes.

## Reusable pattern
For a farewell address: “As I leave [role], remember [shared inheritance]. Preserve [principles]. Beware [predictable dangers]. The future depends on [audience duty].”

## Imitation prompt
Write a farewell speech using Washington’s civic-warning structure. Make the transition a vehicle for principles, warnings, and renewed responsibility.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Making the speech only sentimental.
- Offering warnings without tying them to shared principles.
- Centering the speaker’s legacy over the audience’s duty.
