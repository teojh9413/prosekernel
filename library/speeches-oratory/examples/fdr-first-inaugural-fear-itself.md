# First Inaugural Address

- Author: Franklin D. Roosevelt

## Why this is good
The address names fear without feeding it. Its famous move is to identify the psychological obstacle before turning to diagnosis and action. Crisis oratory needs confidence, but confidence must lead into work, not denial.

## Craft moves
- Names the emotional condition of the audience directly.
- Separates fear from the material problems that require action.
- Uses cadence to create steadiness.
- Balances reassurance with demands for discipline and work.
- Moves from diagnosis to mobilization.

## Structure map
1. Acknowledge the moment and public emotion.
2. Name the chief psychological obstacle.
3. Describe the real problems plainly.
4. State principles for action.
5. Call the audience into disciplined collective work.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: FDR’s inaugural address is a masterclass in crisis leadership rhetoric: calm the public by naming fear, then convert that steadiness into action.

## Reusable pattern
For crisis leadership speech: “We face [real conditions]. The first enemy is [emotional obstacle]. Our principles are [principles]. Our work begins with [actions].”

## Imitation prompt
Write a crisis-leadership address in FDR’s structure. Name fear without amplifying it, diagnose the problem, and mobilize people toward concrete work.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Offering comfort without action.
- Denying the severity of the crisis.
- Using grand cadence to hide vague plans.
