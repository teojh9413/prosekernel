# Gettysburg Address

## Source
- Author: Abraham Lincoln
- URL: https://rmc.library.cornell.edu/gettysburg/good_cause/transcript.htm
- Rights note: Public-domain speech; ProseKernel stores analysis and source link.

## Why this is good
The speech is a masterclass in compression. It does not narrate the battle. It reframes the battlefield as a test of national purpose, then transfers responsibility from the dead to the living. Its power comes from sequence: origin, test, sacrifice, obligation.

## Craft moves
- Begins with shared founding memory rather than the event itself.
- Turns a specific place into a moral test.
- Uses contrast between what speakers can say and what sacrificers already did.
- Repeats key terms to create ceremony without padding.
- Ends by assigning the audience an unfinished task.

## Structure map
1. Founding premise.
2. Present crisis as test of that premise.
3. Recognition of sacrifice.
4. Humility about the limits of speech.
5. Rededication: the living must continue the work.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: Lincoln compresses national history, civil war, death, and democratic renewal into a short ceremonial argument. The speech moves from memory to obligation.

## Reusable pattern
For a high-stakes speech: “We began with [principle]. We are now tested by [crisis]. Others have already paid [cost]. Words are smaller than that cost. Our duty is [unfinished work].”

## Imitation prompt
Write a short public address using the Gettysburg structure: founding principle, present test, sacrifice, humility, renewed duty. Keep it spare and rhythmic.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Overexplaining the context instead of giving the moment meaning.
- Inflating emotion with adjectives rather than structure.
- Ending in vague unity instead of a concrete duty.
