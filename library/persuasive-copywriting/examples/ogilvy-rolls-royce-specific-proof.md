# Ogilvy Rolls-Royce Specific Proof

## Source
- Author: David Ogilvy / Rolls-Royce advertisement
- URL: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Ogilvy_(businessman)
- Rights note: Short headline reference only; use analysis, not full ad copy.

## Why this is good
The famous Rolls-Royce headline works because it sells luxury through a measurable sensory detail rather than empty praise. It does not say “quiet,” “premium,” or “world-class.” It makes the reader infer those things from proof.

## Craft moves
- Uses one concrete product attribute as the whole argument.
- Turns a technical detail into a status signal.
- Lets specificity create desire.
- Avoids superlatives.

## Structure map
1. Unexpected measurement.
2. Implied benefit.
3. Product proof.
4. Reader inference.

## Excerpt or summary
The headline centers on the idea that at highway speed, the loudest sound in the car comes from the electric clock.

## Reusable pattern
Instead of saying “[product] is premium,” find the one detail that forces the reader to conclude it.

## Imitation prompt
Write 10 headlines for this product using Ogilvy-style specific proof. Ban adjectives like best, premium, revolutionary, seamless, and world-class.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Copying the antique luxury tone when the category does not fit.
- Mistaking length for persuasion.
- Using fake specificity without a real product fact.
