# GitLab Handbook — Directly Responsible Individuals

## Source
- Author: GitLab
- URL: https://handbook.gitlab.com/handbook/people-group/directly-responsible-individuals/

## Why this is good
The page turns an abstract management value—ownership—into an operating rule. It clarifies who drives a decision, what authority they have, and how collaboration fits around that authority. Good internal docs reduce repeated debate by making responsibility visible.

## Craft moves
- Defines the term before using it as culture shorthand.
- Assigns decision rights, not just task ownership.
- Separates consultation from final accountability.
- Makes the process reusable across teams.
- Uses handbook-style prose that can be referenced during conflict.

## Structure map
1. Define the role or operating concept.
2. Explain why the organization needs it.
3. State responsibilities and decision rights.
4. Describe collaboration expectations.
5. Provide examples or links for application.

## Excerpt or summary
Summary: GitLab’s DRI guidance documents how one person owns a decision or outcome while still consulting relevant stakeholders. The craft lesson is to make responsibility explicit enough that execution can proceed.

## Reusable pattern
For internal operating docs: “For [area/decision], the DRI is [role/person]. They are accountable for [outcome], must consult [stakeholders], and have authority to [decision]. Escalate when [condition].”

## Imitation prompt
Write an internal ownership doc in GitLab handbook style. Define the role, decision rights, consultation expectations, escalation path, and examples. Optimize for fewer repeated meetings.

## Anti-patterns to avoid
- Saying “the team owns this” when nobody has final accountability.
- Writing culture slogans without execution rules.
- Confusing consensus with consultation.
