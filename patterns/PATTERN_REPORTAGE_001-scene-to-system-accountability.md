# PATTERN_REPORTAGE_001 — Scene to system accountability

## Use when
Use this pattern for reported features, investigations, profiles, field reports, institutional critiques, customer stories, and any article where observed detail must reveal a larger system.

## Reader situation
The reader may not trust abstract claims about a problem. They need to see the system operating through people, scenes, procedures, incentives, and consequences before accepting the broader argument.

## Structure
1. Enter through one concrete person, scene, place, document, or observed moment.
2. Establish how the writer knows: access, method, interview, observation, record, or sequence.
3. Let details accumulate before interpretation.
4. Show the repeated procedure or institutional logic behind the scene.
5. Widen from individual experience to public consequence.
6. Name the accountability question: who knew, who decided, who benefited, who was harmed, what must change.

## Why it works
Reportage earns authority by making claims inspectable. Scene gives the reader sensory and procedural evidence; system analysis gives those details meaning. The strongest version avoids both empty outrage and shapeless anecdote.

## Anti-patterns
- Starting with outrage before evidence.
- Treating the reporter’s experience as the whole point.
- Using one anecdote to overclaim without showing a pattern.
- Replacing observation with adjectives like “shocking,” “broken,” or “troubling.”
- Burying the public-interest question under narrative color.

## Agent instruction
Draft reportage by separating “seen” from “claimed.” First list scenes, documents, quotes, or observed procedures. Then write the system claim those details support. If the scene does not reveal a larger mechanism, keep reporting before concluding.
