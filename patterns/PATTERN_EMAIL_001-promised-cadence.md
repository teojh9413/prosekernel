# PATTERN_EMAIL_001 — Promised cadence email

## Use when
Use this pattern for welcome emails, newsletters, launch sequences, founder updates, and recurring editorial products where trust depends on expectation-setting.

## Reader situation
The reader has given attention or permission but is not yet committed. They need to know what they will receive, why it matters, how often it will arrive, and what one action to take next.

## Structure
1. Confirm the relationship or subscription in one direct sentence.
2. State the reader promise: what useful change the email will reliably create.
3. Set cadence and format so the reader can anticipate the experience.
4. Give a sample of the value or editorial lens.
5. Ask for one next action: read, reply, whitelist, click, or expect the next issue.

## Why it works
Email is permission-based. A promised cadence reduces uncertainty and turns future sends from interruptions into expected value. The structure also protects the writer from overstuffing the email with every possible benefit.

## Anti-patterns
- Thanking the reader, then immediately pitching everything.
- Hiding cadence or format until after generic brand copy.
- Asking for multiple actions in the first email.
- Making the newsletter sound important without saying what the reader gets.
- Confusing voice with jokes that slow down the promise.

## Agent instruction
For any email sequence, write the promise before the prose. Fill this sentence first: “Every [cadence], you will get [specific value] so you can [reader outcome].” Then draft the email around that promise and remove competing CTAs.
