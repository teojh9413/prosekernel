# PATTERN_STRATEGY_001 — Decision-grade principles

## Use when
Use this pattern for strategy memos, founder letters, culture docs, internal operating principles, category theses, and executive writing where the goal is not merely to sound smart but to guide future decisions.

## Reader situation
The reader needs a frame they can use under uncertainty. They may agree with broad values already, but broad values do not tell them what to do when tradeoffs appear.

## Structure
1. Name the principle in plain, memorable language.
2. Define the tradeoff the principle governs.
3. Show what the principle makes the organization do.
4. Show what the principle makes the organization refuse.
5. Connect the principle to a repeatable operating consequence.
6. If possible, include one concrete example or boundary case.

## Why it works
Strategic writing becomes useful when it constrains action. A decision-grade principle turns a belief into a filter: it helps readers choose, prioritize, reject, and explain. Without explicit tradeoffs, strategy collapses into decorative values.

## Anti-patterns
- Writing values that nobody could disagree with.
- Saying “we prioritize X” without naming what loses to X.
- Confusing a slogan with an operating rule.
- Using executive abstraction instead of concrete decision consequences.
- Listing many principles without showing how they change behavior.

## Agent instruction
When drafting strategic writing, force each principle through this test: “What decision would this change?” If the answer is unclear, add a tradeoff, refusal, or operating consequence until the principle becomes usable.
