# PATTERN_INTERNAL_001 — Explicit ownership operating doc

## Use when
Use this pattern for internal handbooks, SOPs, decision records, ownership docs, escalation paths, one-pagers, and team operating agreements where repeated ambiguity slows execution.

## Reader situation
The reader is inside the organization and needs to know who decides, who contributes, what process to follow, and when to escalate. They do not need inspirational language; they need fewer meetings and less ambiguity.

## Structure
1. Define the operating concept or role in one sentence.
2. State the scope: what decisions, systems, or outcomes this covers.
3. Assign ownership: accountable person or role, not a vague group.
4. Separate authority, responsibility, consultation, and execution.
5. Define escalation or exception conditions.
6. Provide examples so teams can apply the rule without reinterpreting it.
7. Link to related docs or artifacts.

## Why it works
Internal writing is infrastructure. It works when it reduces coordination cost. The key move is turning culture shorthand into operational rules: who owns the outcome, who must be consulted, and who has final decision rights.

## Anti-patterns
- “The team owns this” when nobody has final accountability.
- Consensus language that hides decision authority.
- Process docs without examples or escalation paths.
- Values statements pretending to be operating instructions.
- Writing for legal defensibility instead of team usability.

## Agent instruction
When writing an internal ops doc, fill this template first: “For [scope], [role/person] owns [outcome]. They decide [decisions], consult [stakeholders], escalate when [condition], and document [artifact].” Then expand only what helps execution.
