# PATTERN_PROOF_001 — Specific proof ladder

## Use when
Use this pattern when a draft makes a claim that needs credibility: product copy, positioning, technical explanation, strategic memos, crisis notes, or any sentence that asks the reader for trust.

## Reader situation
The reader is skeptical or overloaded. They have seen too many claims like “powerful,” “trusted,” “easy,” “innovative,” or “world-class.” They do not need louder confidence; they need a concrete reason to believe.

## Structure
1. Name the claim in plain language.
2. Replace adjectives with a specific observable fact: number, source, behavior, example, constraint, timeline, or mechanism.
3. Explain what that fact proves for this reader.
4. If possible, show the proof in the form the reader can feel: before/after, spec-to-benefit translation, timeline, demo, or concrete tradeoff.
5. Cut any credibility gesture that does not do evidentiary work.

## Why it works
Weak writing asks for trust before earning it. Specific proof lowers the reader’s cognitive burden because it gives them something to inspect. The strongest proof does not merely decorate a claim; it changes the reader’s model of why the claim is true.

## Anti-patterns
- “Trusted by teams everywhere” with no named evidence.
- “Research shows” without source, mechanism, or implication.
- Stacking logos, adjectives, or awards instead of making one concrete proof move.
- Adding numbers that sound precise but do not prove the claim.
- Leaving the reader to infer why the evidence matters.

## Agent instruction
When revising a claim, identify the exact trust request. Replace at least one adjective or abstract noun with a named proof marker, then write the implication in reader-language: “This matters because…” If no proof exists, weaken the claim until it is honest.
