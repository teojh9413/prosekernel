# PATTERN_PERSUASION_001 — Awareness-matched argument

## Use when
Use this pattern for landing pages, sales emails, launch copy, fundraising narratives, product pages, ads, and any persuasive writing where different readers are at different levels of readiness.

## Reader situation
The reader is somewhere on an awareness ladder: unaware, problem-aware, solution-aware, product-aware, or most-aware. A message fails when it asks for an action before the reader has accepted the previous belief.

## Structure
1. Diagnose the reader’s current belief and awareness stage.
2. Define the next belief the writing must create.
3. Choose the message job for that stage:
   - unaware: reveal the problem through pain or contrast.
   - problem-aware: name the cause and stakes.
   - solution-aware: explain the mechanism or approach.
   - product-aware: prove this offer is the right instance.
   - most-aware: remove friction and ask clearly.
4. Match proof burden and CTA intensity to readiness.
5. Handle the next objection, not every possible objection.

## Why it works
Persuasion is diagnostic before rhetorical. Strong copy does not say everything; it says the next thing the reader is ready to believe. This prevents two common failures: educating buyers who are ready to act, and selling to readers who do not yet feel the problem.

## Anti-patterns
- Selling before the reader believes the problem matters.
- Teaching basics to a reader who already wants the offer.
- Using urgency before desire exists.
- Handling every objection instead of the next objection.
- Writing from the company’s desired CTA rather than the reader’s current belief.

## Agent instruction
Before drafting persuasive copy, write two sentences: “The reader currently believes ____. After this piece, they should believe ____.” Then choose hook, proof, and CTA intensity based on that gap. If the CTA feels premature, move one awareness stage earlier.
