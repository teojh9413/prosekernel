# PATTERN_BRAND_001 — Worldview positioning

## Use when
Use this pattern for brand positioning, category pages, manifestos, about pages, product narratives, and launch copy where the product needs to stand for a way of seeing the world, not just a list of features.

## Reader situation
The reader can compare alternatives, but they do not yet know why this product or company should matter. They need a frame that makes the offering feel inevitable for a specific tribe, mission, or future.

## Structure
1. Name the world as it is changing or the old category assumption that is breaking.
2. State the belief or mission that guides the company.
3. Show the product as a consequence of that belief, not as a random feature bundle.
4. Make the audience identity clear: who this is for and what they are trying to become.
5. Compress the positioning into a phrase, contrast, or promise that can travel.

## Why it works
Strong positioning gives features a worldview. It tells readers what game the company is playing and why the product choices belong together. The best version creates both differentiation and belonging: “this is for people like us, solving the problem this way.”

## Examples
- `library/brand-positioning/examples/apple-think-different.md` — positions brand as worldview and identity rather than feature list.
- `library/brand-positioning/examples/patagonia-home-planet-mission.md` — makes mission a constraint on company decisions.
- `library/brand-positioning/examples/linear-purpose-built-product-development-positioning.md` — uses narrow purpose and product taste as positioning.
- `library/brand-positioning/examples/github-where-the-world-builds-software.md` — makes the platform feel like the default place a tribe does its work.
- `library/brand-positioning/examples/airbnb-belong-anywhere.md` — reframes a transaction category around emotional belonging.

## Anti-patterns
- Positioning as a pile of features and adjectives.
- Copying category language instead of naming a distinct worldview.
- Claiming a mission that does not constrain product or business choices.
- Making the audience everyone.
- Creating a slogan that sounds good but does not clarify the product’s role.

## Agent instruction
Before writing brand copy, fill in: “We believe [worldview], so we built [product choice] for [specific audience] who want [desired future].” Use that sentence as the spine; make features prove the worldview instead of replacing it.
