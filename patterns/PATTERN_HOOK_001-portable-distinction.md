# PATTERN_HOOK_001 — Portable distinction hook

## Use when
Use this pattern when readers feel a recurring problem but lack a clean label for it. It works for essays, social posts, product narratives, strategy memos, and category positioning.

## Reader situation
The reader has experienced the pain, but the pain feels messy. They may blame the wrong surface cause. A strong distinction gives them language they can reuse, remember, and share.

## Structure
1. Start with the conflict the reader already recognizes.
2. Reframe it as two named modes, forces, schedules, incentives, or worldviews.
3. Show one concrete consequence of confusing the two.
4. Give the reader a rule for deciding which side they are in.
5. Close by making the distinction portable enough to quote or apply elsewhere.

## Why it works
A portable distinction creates compression without flattening thought. It turns a vague feeling into a handle. Once readers have the handle, they can diagnose their own situation and repeat the idea to others.

- `library/viral-social/examples/paul-graham-makers-schedule.md` — names maker schedule vs manager schedule so workplace friction becomes legible.
- `library/viral-social/examples/a16z-software-is-eating-the-world-thesis.md` — names a broad economic shift in a phrase that became shorthand.
- `library/strategic-intelligent/examples/basecamp-shape-up-appetite.md` — distinguishes appetite from estimate to change how product scope is discussed.
- `library/brand-positioning/examples/sqlite-small-fast-reliable.md` — compresses a product philosophy into memorable constraints.
- `library/technical-explanatory/examples/diataxis-documentation-framework.md` — separates documentation modes by user need.

## Anti-patterns
- Starting with a generic “here are three tips” frame.
- Coining a clever term that does not clarify a real tension.
- Creating a false binary where the reader needs nuance.
- Naming the distinction but failing to show a practical consequence.
- Writing a hook that is shareable but not useful.

## Agent instruction
Before drafting the opening, ask: “What distinction would make this problem easier to see?” Name the two sides in simple language, show one consequence, then use the rest of the piece to make the distinction useful rather than merely clever.
