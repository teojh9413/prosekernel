# PATTERN_SPEECH_001 — Shared principle to renewed duty

## Use when
Use this pattern for speeches, public remarks, founder keynotes, ceremonies, movement statements, and high-stakes addresses where the audience must connect a present moment to a larger obligation.

## Reader situation
The audience already knows the occasion matters, but they may not know what meaning to take from it. They need orientation, moral scale, and a next duty without being drowned in context or sentiment.

## Structure
1. Begin with a shared origin, principle, promise, or founding memory.
2. Name the present moment as a test of that principle.
3. Recognize the cost, sacrifice, work, or stakes already present.
4. Show humility about what words can and cannot do.
5. Transfer responsibility to the audience: what remains unfinished.
6. End with a compressed duty, not a vague emotion.

## Why it works
A strong speech turns an event into obligation. It does not merely describe what happened; it tells the audience what the moment asks of them. Compression and cadence make the duty memorable enough to carry beyond the room.

## Anti-patterns
- Beginning with boilerplate gratitude or context dump.
- Inflating emotion with adjectives instead of moral sequence.
- Ending with “unity,” “hope,” or “moving forward” without a duty.
- Making the speaker the hero rather than the audience’s obligation.
- Explaining the whole situation instead of giving the moment meaning.

## Agent instruction
Before drafting a speech, write the duty sentence first: “Because [shared principle] is being tested by [present moment], we must [unfinished work].” Then build the speech backward through origin, test, cost, humility, and renewed duty.
