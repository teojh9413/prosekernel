# Anti-Slop Doctrine

This file is the immune system of Humanprint.

## Core enemy

AI slop is not just bad phrasing. It is often **specificity fading while importance gets inflated**.

A concrete fact becomes a generic claim. A real mechanism becomes “transformation.” A weak source list becomes “notability.” A padded ending becomes “future prospects.”

## Immediate fail phrases

Rewrite or delete:

- in today's fast-paced world
- ever-evolving landscape
- delve into
- unlock
- unleash
- leverage
- game-changer
- seamless
- robust
- cutting-edge
- revolutionary, unless proven
- transformative, unless proven
- it is important to note
- in conclusion
- this article explores
- stands as / serves as
- testament to
- pivotal / crucial / significant / key, when unsupported
- underscores / highlights / reflects broader
- enduring legacy
- valuable insights

## Wikipedia AI-writing tells

Source: Wikipedia’s “Signs of AI writing.”

Use it as a field guide, not as a detector. The signs are surface symptoms of deeper problems.

### 1. Legacy inflation

AI often claims ordinary things are pivotal, enduring, significant, or part of a broader trend.

Fix: show concrete consequence or cut the claim.

### 2. Superficial analysis

AI attaches vague participle phrases:

- highlighting...
- underscoring...
- reflecting...
- symbolizing...
- contributing to...
- fostering...

Fix: say what happened, who did it, and why it matters.

### 3. Vague attribution

Weak:
> Many experts believe...

Strong:
> In the 1997 shareholder letter, Bezos argues that long-term market leadership requires prioritizing customer value over short-term accounting appearance.

### 4. Source-list theater

Listing media outlets is not proof. Explain what each source establishes.

### 5. Formulaic endings

Avoid “challenges and future prospects” endings unless the piece genuinely needs that structure.

Close with:

- a decision
- a consequence
- a concrete image
- a useful synthesis
- a direct ask

### 6. Formatting cosplay

AI often uses bold headers, vertical lists, em dashes, and tables to look organized. Formatting must serve thought.

## Final anti-slop questions

Ask before publishing:

1. Did we replace specific facts with generic importance?
2. Did we add “broader significance” without proof?
3. Did we use “highlighting/underscoring/reflecting” instead of saying what happened?
4. Did we cite sources without explaining what they prove?
5. Did we end with padded summary instead of a useful close?
6. Did formatting make the draft look organized while thought stayed weak?
7. Could the author explain why each paragraph exists?
